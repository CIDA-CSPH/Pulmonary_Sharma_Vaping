PCA Analysis

PCA analysis was completed used the built in functions in DESeq2, namely plotPCA(). This function was used with gene counts with variance stabilizing transformation (VST) applied, the group of interest was infant sex (intgroup), and all other options were set at defaults.

PCA Plot of All Genes

pca_all <- plotPCA(vst(dds), intgroup = "infant_sex", returnData = TRUE)
pca_all$PID <- sub('.', '', pca_all$name)
pca_all$infant_sex <- ifelse(pca_all$infant_sex == "2", "male", "female")
percentVarA <- round(100 * attr(pca_all, "percentVar"))

qplot(PC1, PC2, color = infant_sex, data = pca_all) + theme_bw() +
  geom_text_repel(data = subset(pca_all, subset = PID %in% c("21509", "21572","21344")), aes(label = PID), color = "black") +
  labs(x = paste0("PC1: ",percentVarA[1],"% variance"), y = paste0("PC2: ",percentVarA[2],"% variance"), color = "Infant Sex")

From the plot above PC2 separates the subjects by infant sex fairly well. Our three subjects of interest labeled, 21344 is grouped with the male subjects, 21509 is closer to the male group then the female group, and 21572 is in the female group. There are though a few female and male subjects in that middle area that could go either way.

Genes on the Sex Chromosomes

To continue looking at this possible misidentified sex the genes located on the sex chromosome will be looked at. Starting with just looking at the genes on the Y chromosome exclusively. In Ensembl, only genes unique to the Y chromosome are labeled on the Y chromosome, genes on the parts of the Y chromosome that are analogous to the X chromosome are included only on the X chromosome.

Y Chromosome

There are 14 genes on the Y chromosome, of these 9 are protein coding.
